We've Been Building
Forecasting Models Wrong —
Cast-R1 and the Case for
Agentic Time Series

Every forecasting model in quant finance is built on a silent assumption: prediction is a single-pass problem. A February 2026 arXiv paper challenges that from first principles — and the implications go further than the paper admits.

Published March 2026
Reading time ~6 min
Paper arXiv:2602.13802
LLMs Forecasting Architecture Paper Breakdown Quant ML

There is an assumption so deep in quantitative finance that nobody states it out loud anymore. Every forecasting model — LSTM, Transformer, PatchTST, TimeMixer, N-BEATS — is built on the same premise: you feed in historical data, you get a forecast out. One pass. Done.

This is so obvious it seems like physics. Of course forecasting is a single-pass problem. What else would it be?

A paper published in February 2026 by researchers at the University of Science and Technology of China argues, carefully and with evidence, that this framing is wrong. Not slightly off — structurally wrong. And once you see the argument, it's hard to unsee it.

// Paper
Cast-R1: Learning Tool-Augmented Sequential Decision Policies for Time Series Forecasting
Tao, Cheng, Jiang, Gao, Zhang, Liu  ·  arXiv:2602.13802  ·  February 14, 2026  ·  arxiv.org/abs/2602.13802

Reformulates time series forecasting as a sequential decision-making problem. Introduces memory-based state management and a tool-augmented agentic workflow in which an agent autonomously interacts with a modular forecasting toolkit. Trained via supervised fine-tuning followed by multi-turn reinforcement learning.

// The argument

The paper opens with an observation that any experienced quant will recognise immediately: expert forecasters don't actually forecast in one shot.

They examine historical patterns. They characterise the data — trend, seasonality, regime. They select a model, check residuals, identify anomalies, pull in contextual information, and revise. When new evidence surfaces, they update the forecast. This is iterative, evidence-driven, and deeply sequential. It looks nothing like the forward pass of a neural network.

The paper's core claim: "High-quality forecasting involves a series of interdependent decisions rather than a one-shot model inference." Cast-R1 proposes to learn this entire decision process — not just the mapping from input to output — as a trainable system.

Cast-R1 operationalises this through three components:

Memory-based state management. The system maintains a running state across decision steps, accumulating contextual evidence — data quality assessments, structural characterisations, anomaly flags, model outputs — rather than discarding intermediate reasoning between steps.

A modular forecasting toolkit. Rather than a single model, Cast-R1 has access to a set of callable tools: data quality assessment, global statistical characterisation, structural and dynamic analysis, event-level summarisation, residual diagnostics, and model invocation. The agent decides which tools to call, in what order, and how to synthesise their outputs.

Two-stage training. First, supervised fine-tuning on expert forecasting trajectories — the sequence of decisions a good analyst would make. Then multi-turn reinforcement learning, where the agent optimises the full decision sequence against forecasting accuracy.

// Why this matters more for finance than general time series

The paper is written for general time series forecasting. But the argument lands harder in finance than anywhere else, for a specific reason: financial time series are not stationary, not Gaussian, and not generated by a fixed process.

Regime changes — from low-volatility trending markets to high-volatility mean-reverting ones — are abrupt and unpredictable. Events like central bank announcements, earnings surprises, or geopolitical shocks create discontinuities that no look-back window fully captures. A single-pass model trained on historical data has no mechanism to detect that the regime has changed mid-forecast and update accordingly. A sequential agent does.

This matters most in macro and fixed income, where regime shifts are more frequent and more consequential than in equity momentum strategies. A global macro fund running a single-pass LSTM on yield curve dynamics is implicitly assuming the data-generating process is stable enough that one look at the history tells you everything. It rarely is.

// What the paper actually shows

Fig 2 — Cast-R1 Modular Forecasting Toolkit

The experimental results show Cast-R1 outperforming single-pass baselines across standard time series benchmarks. But the more interesting finding is qualitative: the agent learns to use the toolkit in ways the researchers didn't explicitly program. It discovers, through reinforcement learning, that running residual diagnostics before invoking a second model improves outcomes. It learns when to skip tools that add noise rather than signal.

This is the part that should interest quant practitioners. The model isn't just better at forecasting — it's learning something about the process of forecasting. That's a fundamentally different thing to optimise.

// The honest critique

The paper has real limitations that deserve naming.

The benchmarks are general, not financial. Cast-R1 is evaluated on ETTh1, ETTm1, Weather, and similar standard datasets. These are well-behaved compared to financial time series. No fat tails, no regime switches, no adversarial microstructure. Whether the agentic loop adds value when the data-generating process is genuinely adversarial remains untested.

The toolkit is fixed at training time. The agent learns which tools to call, but it can't extend the toolkit at inference. In production finance, you often need to pull in a new data source or apply a model that didn't exist when the system was trained. A truly adaptive system needs dynamic tool registration — not solved here.

Latency is unstated. Multi-step agentic loops are slower than single-pass inference. For daily or weekly macro forecasting this doesn't matter. For anything closer to the close of a trading session, it might.

Explainability is a byproduct, not a design goal. The sequential decision trace gives you something to audit — which tools were called, what the intermediate state looked like — but the paper doesn't frame this as an explainability contribution. In regulated environments, this could actually be the most valuable property of the architecture. It's an opportunity the paper misses.

// The broader claim I'd make

The quant community has spent thirty years optimising model architectures — better attention mechanisms, better positional encodings, better training objectives. Cast-R1 suggests we've been optimising the wrong thing. The architecture of the model matters less than the architecture of the decision process around it.

This is not a small claim. It means that the right frontier for research isn't "a better transformer for financial time series" — it's "a better protocol for how a system gathers evidence and revises its forecast." The Cast-R1 framework is an early stab at formalising that protocol.

The finance-specific version of this idea — an agentic forecasting system with access to macro data feeds, event calendars, central bank communications, and real-time residual monitoring — is not science fiction. It's an engineering problem. And it's more tractable than the next 2% of improvement on a benchmark model.
